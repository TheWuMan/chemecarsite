Battery

The Battery Subteam of Cornell ChemE Car is a team of dedicated researchers looking to push the boundaries of battery technology. Our members are looking at ways of applying chemical engineering principles to control different aspects of the anode, cathode, and design fabrication of batteries with the hopes of achieving a high electrical output.

Current interests are twofold. One is to create a zinc-alkaline battery that utilizes a novel acrylic casing, mimicking a commercial battery. While developing a consistent anode gel proved to be a slight setback, recent results have been promising. Two is focused on finally bringing the lithium ion to competition. This would make Cornell one of the few (if any) teams to bring an effective secondary battery to competition. For both projects, parameters are changed in order to optimize discharge capacity, discharge rate, voltage output, and current output. Overall, we are an innovative team seeking to gain high level technical skills.

  • Subteam Leader: James Dong
  • Founding Date: Fall 2010

Biological Systems

The Biological Systems Sub-team of Cornell ChemE Car is primarily a research and development team where the focus is on identifying and optimizing biologically-sourced chemical reactions to power a competition car. Current projects study the potential for gas-producing reactions to generate large pressure gradients. The team is currently working with a catalase and peroxide reaction.

  • Subteam Leader: Jennifer Li
  • Founding Date: Spring 2013

Fuel Cell

The Fuel Cell subteam began in 2006 as one of the main power teams for Cornell ChemE Car. The team has been built on a foundation of success after Bender v.4.0 won Nationals in 2008 and doing consistently well at both regional and national ChemE Car competitions since. The main focus of the subteam has been to assemble a car that runs on power generated by a hydrogen powered PEM fuel cell. The Fuel Cell subteam has worked to innovate and create more efficient and consistent use of this power source. The current car setup that the team is working on focuses on our own hydrogen-generation reactions in a vessel that then transports the hydrogen produced through the manifold where it is regulated in order to power our 10 cell-stack PEM fuel cell. While working hands-on with the car, team members also get R&D experience, since our car is always improving. The end goal, as with the rest of our team, is to bring home even more wins from regional and national ChemE Car competitions.

  • Subteam Leader: Jeese Reeves
  • Founding Date: Fall 2010

Pressure

The Pressure team is working to make a car that runs on a pressure-producing reaction. Our current reaction produces carbon dioxide from citric acid and baking soda, which can be run through an air motor to power the car.

  • Subteam Leader: Alexandra Vito
  • Founding Date: Fall 2011

TEG

The TEG Subteam of Cornell ChemE Car is the thermo-electric generation team. As the name implies, this subteam exploits heat to generate electricity and ultimately power the ChemE car. TEG is a new team that was formed in Spring 2013 and is in the research and development stage. We are looking to develop a consistent way to power the car through a clean and safe chemical reaction. This semester we are exploring designs that utilize a TEG plate to convert the heat from an exothermic reaction into electricity.

  • Subteam Leader: Jennifer Bushnell
  • Founding Date: Spring 2013

Potions

The Potions Subteam is responsible for controlling the stopping mechanism of our Chemical Engineering Car. Through the Iodine Clock Reaction, we are able to accurately and precisely control the time at which our car will stop moving by integrating a light sensor and circuit. By calibrating our reaction during weekly time trials and during the competition, we can quickly, accurately, and efficiently judge the ratios of our chemicals before we initiate the reaction at the starting line. During our weekly meetings, members will learn about the reaction and its kinetics, help construct calibration curves, and participate in weekly wet-lab competitions judges on accuracy.

  • Subteam Leader: Nicholas Ornitz
  • Founding Date: Fall 2010

Mechanical

The mechanical subteam is responsible for designing and building the chassis and drivetrains for each of the cars. Using CAD software, members design each part and carry it through to production. The mechanical team also uses rapid prototyping technology such as laser cutting and 3D printing to manufacture parts quickly and precisely. By simultaneously engaging in research and development, the members of the mechanical team are constantly looking for ways to improve its processes and systems while maintaining a safe, efficient, and accurate delivery of mechanical motion.

  • Subteam Leader: Ashley Fung
  • Founding Date: Fall 2010

Business

Business is the newest subteam in ChemE Car, formed from the merger of the Info and Finance sub-teams. Business is part of the administrative division of ChemE Car. We focus on recruitment, public relations, sponsorship, data maintenance, outreach, and inter-team communication. Business members collect information on what each subteam is working on, compile monthly newsletters to keep our alumni and sponsors in the loop, and provide opportunities for all members to participate in engaging community service activities that spread the word about what ChemE Car is and what we do.

  • Subteam Leader: Pradeep Prathibha
  • Founding Date: Fall 2015

Electronics

The Electronics Subteam of Cornell ChemE Car consists of all kinds of majors, but mainly, anyone who is passionate about learning and making electronics is welcome! The whole team interacts with many of the power subteams - battery, fuel cell, pressure, TEG, and bio - to respond effectively to their needs as well as bond with them. The electronics team is currently working with microcontrollers to improve the calibrations of the cars by collecting data about the speed, voltage, and current and transmitting it through bluetooth to a computer. In addition to working with microcontrollers, the team of course plays around with circuits. We design circuits tailored to each power subteam car on breadboard, and once everything is finalized, we design a small, compact PCB (printed circuit board) to be put on the cars for competition. Lastly, we are always trying to create new stopping mechanisms for other power subteams since each power source operates differently. Our main stopping mechanism circuit used is with the iodine clock reaction where there is a light shining through the potions chamber reaching a photoresistor on the other. Once the iodine clock reaction is completed, the whole solution turns blue-black, drastically decreasing the amount of light shining on the photoresistor. This in turn signals the circuit to cut off power to the motor, stopping the car.

  • Subteam Leader: Christine Soong
  • Founding Date: Fall 2010

Safety

The Safety subteam is responsible for maintaining laboratory standards, safe operation and providing other subteams with advice about safety and competition rules. Each member of the Safety team specializes in one safety aspect and serves as liaison with a power team. They are responsible for attending their respective power team’s meetings, completing specific deliverables, and ensuring that all AIChE’s Safety Rules are adhered to and that proper safety protocols are implemented at all times. The goal of this set up is to provide full immersion, hands-on experience and a comprehensive understanding of the development and construction of the cars. Each car’s specifications and job safety analysis (JSA) are documented in the Engineering Documentation Package (EDP) compiled by safety members.

  • Subteam Leader: Ngan (Sophie) Le
  • Founding Date: Fall 2010
